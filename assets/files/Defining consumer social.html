A framework for social products

Making decisions on individual bets is hard without a framework. I propose that social products can be defined using 4 key attributes:

Connections, Creators, Community, and Core Action.

We can think of this as a 4x4 chart:

Connections Creators Community Core Action
Local Long-tail Explicit Creation
Global Short-tail Implicit Consumption

Connections - by and large, who do users engage with?

  • Local: you interact with your friends on BeReal, Lapse, and Gas
  • Global: you interact with strangers on Twitch, TikTok, and Twitter

Creators - by and large, who drives content creation?

  • Long-tail: the average user drives content creation on BeReal, Lapse, and Gas
  • Short-tail: professional creators drive content creation on Twitch, TikTok, and Twitter

Community - by and large, do users explicitly subdivide by interests?

  • Explicit: you explicitly join friend groups on BeReal, Lapse, and Gas
  • Implicit: TikTok, Twitch, and Twitter algorithms infer user interests

Core Action - by and large, what‚Äôs the main thing that most users do?

  • Creation: For BeReal, Lapse, and Gas, the core action is that everyone creates content
  • Consumption: On TikTok, Twitch, and Twitter, most people just consume content

Why should I care?

  • Nascent social networks like us need to choose 1 attribute within each category.
    • Mature social networks can successfully blur the lines (e.g.¬†current FB is both local and global, current IG serves both long-tail (your friends‚Äô stories) and short-tail (IG influencers) creators); we cannot.
    • As a new social product, it will be impossible to find product-market fit while trying to create multiple product offerings for multiple markets at once. Conflicting forces will create confusing UX and unclear product identity.
  • Different combinations of attributes will give a network its early identity.
  • Some combinations of attributes make sense; some make no sense.
    • If the goal is for every user to create content, the network must be local ‚Äî John Doe‚Äôs random selfie has no value to a stranger, but it can be interesting for his friends.
    • If the goal is for users to interact with strangers, then, given enough scale, professional creators / power users will emerge and drown out the average, low quality creator.
  • By mapping where Mush fits in the framework, we can make good decisions.

Which combination of attributes make sense?

  • Local long-tail network ‚úÖ

    Take Discord as an example. (early FB & Snapchat also loosely fit here)

    1. Connections: Users interact locally (1st degree connections: friends, people you game with)
    1. Creators: Because these are local users within a private, explicit community, creators are necessarily long-tail. Everyone chats together.
    1. Community: Communities can be explicit (discord server)
    1. Core Loop - Long-tail creators both create and consume.

    Local network takeaways:

    • Locally-connected networks tend to be driven by long-tail creators.
    • Long-tail creators both create and consume content
    • Networks that start out Local, get Global as they grow (dominant creators emerge, public figures join, etc) eg. FB, Snapchat, IG
  • Global short-tail network ‚úÖ

    Tiktok/Youtube are examples of globally-connected networks.

    1. Connections: Users interact globally through a powerful For You feed/Suggested Videos
    1. Creators: Due to the nature of a global network, short-tail creators drive content.
    1. Community: Community is implied through content clusters & algorithmic content distribution, not through explicit membership in a community.
    1. Core Loop: Because Short-tail creators drive content, the median user just consumes and is not creating content.

    Global network takeaways:

    • Globally-connected networks tend to be driven by short-tail creators (less local pull for long-tail content).
    • Globally-connected networks tend to have implicit communities due to the lack of strong local forces that bind explicit communities & their tendency to have interest clustering/algo-driven content distribution
    • On globally-connected networks, the median user tends to consume and not create.
  • Global long-tail network ‚ö†Ô∏è

    Global + Long-tail networks are rare. These tend to be content-heavy sites and less-so conventional social networks. Think reddit or 9gag, where everyone posts content (and long-tail comments themselves are content) and the engagement radius is global.

    • A quick note on Reddit as an exception on Community:
      • On the surface, community is explicit (subreddit membership), but default subreddit membership is in large subreddits where ‚Äúcommunity‚Äù is a vague container for millions of users. The spirit of community for the average member may feel more implicit in practice (they don‚Äôt necessarily identity as a ‚Äúredditor‚Äù)

  • Local short-tail network ‚ùå

    This pairing doesn‚Äôt work, or at least I can‚Äôt think of any social network that has that dynamic. Short-tail creators don‚Äôt really have a chance to emerge within a local network, and local networks don‚Äôt have the scale or monetization needed to attract existing big-name creators. Sure, you can have power users within a local network, but in the grand scheme of a social network I would still categorize those users as long-tail users.

Currently, we are trying to be a global long-tail network. This is concerning ‚ö†Ô∏è

  1. Connections: global. Users primarily interact globally through a For You feed & common challenge hub. We do have messaging around ‚Äúfind your squad‚Äù during onboarding, but we haven‚Äôt implemented a growth strategy around friends, nor do we reinforce that messaging throughout the app.
  1. Creators: both? Content is mostly created by the average user, though we do pay short-tail creators to post on Mushroom. I think it‚Äôs hard to truly attract short-tail creators when we don‚Äôt have differentiating creator features and don‚Äôt have scale.
  1. Community: implicit. You can follow anyone and we don‚Äôt draw hard lines on groups.

  1. Core Action: both? We want users both creating (posts/challenges) and consuming (feed).

Imagine if BeReal showed you candids from strangers all over the world. You‚Äôd have no reason to care. Vice versa, imagine if TikTok only showed you posts from your friends. You‚Äôd be pretty ticked off because you know TikTok has so much more global content to offer. This contradiction in attributes may be what we have right now.

We arrived at our current set of attributes through a grab-bag of features, and this is the critical time for us to come to a strategic consensus. Do we want users to make friends on our platform and create long-tail content for each other? Or do we want users to passively consume short-tail content from strangers across the world? As soon as we align on who we‚Äôre serving and what problem we want to solve for them, figuring out whether each tactical bet is a good idea becomes a much easier task.

We are connecting users globally while also focusing on long-tail creators, but most social networks connect locally early on if they‚Äôre focusing long-tail creators. Local connections is a great way to start a nascent social network.

  • Incumbents like IG, FB, Snapchat, Discord focused on local connections before pivoting to global after reaching scale.
  • New trending social apps are also focusing on local: Gas, BeReal, Journal, Geojam, Sush, etc.
  • One of our arguments against going local is that local networks can never support ad monetization at scale. This may be true, but many incumbents show us that it‚Äôs possible to pivot from local to global once you have users.

Finally, some actionable questions for us

  • Can we revisit features that promote local connection and long-tail creators?
    • Guilds for people who play similar games?
    • Find your gaming squad and auto-ping them every time you start playing?
    • Generate content from gaming session rich data and auto-tag your friends?
    • Find gaming friends nearby?
    • Best friend oriented features?
  • Or, do we think the psychology of gamers uniquely enable a global + long-tail approach, unlike other platforms?
    • Maybe game clip content is interesting to a global audience whereas FB family photos are purely local?
  • If we are sure we want to also attract short-tail creators, how do we incentivize them aside from pure cash?
    • Challenges have the potential to be a differentiated creator feature: eg. Roblox devs/influencers could post challenges for the community and also post short-form video content around those challenges
  • And the strategic question: what characteristics do we want Mushroom to have?
